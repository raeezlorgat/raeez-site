Raeez Lorgat

Apartheid fell; I left South Africa for the UK. Early life lesson: institutions are agreements—and agreements can be rewritten.

In 2007 I won Intel ISEF; MIT's Lincoln Laboratory later named minor planet 23122 Lorgat after me. This was possible because as a teen I built raytracers and game engines and short films, chasing geometry not because it was useful, but because it was beautiful. MIT followed.

At MIT I met Patrick Collison. Freshman year we bounced between California and Massachusetts, and ended one week flying up the Pacific coast in a small plane we’d pilot ourselves—Patrick’s first international trip, my first flight ever.

I dropped out with the Collison brothers, moved to Palo Alto, and helped build Stripe. We cold‑called banks and wrote the code that would process hundreds of billions.

Stripe made the next problem obvious: the exponential friction in global compliance. I spent the next decade at MIT and the Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics working on arithmetic geometry, factorization algebras, and the geometry of quantum field theory. The mathematics I study—how local structures compose into global ones—shapes how I think about institutional design.

Now I’m building Mass : a new kind of decentralized network where the nodes are sovereign jurisdictions and assets become programmable, carrying their own compliance. Live in the US, Cayman, BVI, and the Special Economic Zone Próspera ZEDE in Honduras. Combined asset volume passed $1B in 2025.

Momentum is the fund building this ecosystem. In 2026, I'm moving our HQ and home to Abu Dhabi.
